# Living With High Unemployment: Tools for a Post-Work Society

Research compiled March 2026 — focusing not on preventing unemployment, but on the social infrastructure needed when a large fraction of the population is permanently displaced from traditional employment.

---

## 1. Income Security Without Employment

### Universal Basic Income (UBI) & Guaranteed Income (GI)

**Current state:** No country has implemented nationwide UBI as of 2026, but 160+ pilots have been conducted globally (Stanford Basic Income Lab). Most real-world programs are technically Guaranteed Basic Income (GBI) — means-tested, phased out as income rises.

**Key evidence:**
- **Swiss 2016 referendum:** CHF 2,500/month UBI rejected 76.9% to 23.1%. Federal Council estimated gross cost CHF 208 bn/year, net CHF 25 bn after replacing existing transfers. This remains the political baseline.
- **NBER 2024 study (ORUS):** $1,000/month payments reduced labor supply by 1.3–1.4 hours/week, 3.9pp less likely to be employed. Decline was on the *extensive margin* (people leaving work entirely).
- **Cambridge (Jaimovich et al.):** UBI at 10% of average income + existing UI could *increase* output 1.27% and reduce unemployment 0.5pp. Income elasticity of labor force participation: −0.1 to −0.3.
- **Wales pilot (2022–2026):** £1,600/month for care leavers turning 18. Mid-trial: improved mental health, more balanced lives, pursuit of education.
- **U.S. Guaranteed Income Pilot Program Act 2025:** Proposed 3-year pilot with monthly payments equal to local fair market rent for 2-bedroom home. $495M authorized for FY2026–2030.

**The labor supply tension:** UBI consistently reduces labor force participation in experiments. But in a high-unemployment future, this may be a *feature* not a bug — fewer people competing for fewer jobs, with income floor preventing destitution.

**Fiscal reality for Switzerland:** At CHF 2,500/month for ~7M adults = ~CHF 210 bn gross. Current total social expenditure is CHF 179.5 bn. Net cost after replacing existing transfers is the key figure — estimated at CHF 25 bn (2016 estimate), roughly 3% of GDP.

### Beyond UBI: Sovereign Wealth Funds, AI Taxation & Universal Basic Services

UBI is only one funding mechanism. Several complementary approaches are being developed:

**Sovereign Wealth Funds (SWFs) for AI dividends:**
- Convergence Analysis (2025) proposes SWFs with three roles: **Lead** (invest in frontier AI), **Own** (acquire equity in AI companies for public benefit), **Share** (distribute returns as universal basic dividends).
- SWFs can also serve as stabilization funds — smoothing tax revenues and labor shocks from rapid automation.
- U.S. Executive Order (Feb 2025): President Trump signed an order directing creation of a U.S. sovereign wealth fund "for the sole benefit of American citizens."
- SWFs invested $46 bn in AI ventures in the first 8 months of 2025 alone.

**Sam Altman's "American Equity Fund":**
- Large AI companies and landholders contribute ~2.5% of their value annually to a fund distributed to all citizens — effectively public ownership of the automated economy.

**Data royalty models:**
- Treat public data as a natural resource. AI companies pay royalties for training data, proceeds distributed via SWF.

**AI/Automation taxation:**
- Consumption taxes and land value taxes are most promising in an automated economy (purchases still happen even without human labor).
- Direct "robot taxes" face definitional challenges — what counts as "automation" is hard to draw a line around.
- MIT research (Costinot & Werning 2022): optimal robot tax should be "modest" to avoid distortions. Swiss-specific constraint: FDI elasticity and cantonal tax competition make this the least viable Swiss option.

**Universal Basic Services (UBS):**
- Alternative to cash transfers: provide free/subsidized healthcare, education, housing, transport, digital access.
- Hybrid models combining modest cash (UBI) + universal services may be more politically viable and fiscally efficient than pure UBI.

**Fiscal risk:** If the "AI bubble" bursts (analysts warned by late 2025 that AI revenue hasn't matched investment hype), funding sources for equity-based redistribution could evaporate. Diversification of funding mechanisms is critical.

## 2. Mental Health & Social Cohesion Infrastructure

### The psychological crisis of mass unemployment

Work provides far more than income — it structures time, confers identity, creates social bonds, and gives purpose. Loss of employment correlates with depression, anxiety, low self-esteem, and stress-related illness across all social strata.

**Key findings:**
- **2025 systematic review:** Unemployment causes measurable increases in depression, anxiety, and psychological distress. Re-employment reverses most mental health effects, confirming causality.
- **Social cohesion as buffer:** Neighbourhood social cohesion has a *greater impact* on mental health than economic deprivation alone. Trust, belonging, and feeling valued are the strongest predictors of wellbeing.
- **EU policy direction:** Europe is working toward a binding directive on Mental Health and Work (target: ~2029), recognizing that mass alienation feeds extremism and populism.
- **The "status syndrome":** Psychological experience of inequality and lack of control over one's life drives mental health outcomes more than absolute deprivation.

### Policy implications for Switzerland:
- Switzerland's cantonal Sozialhilfe system guarantees a "dignified existence" but is designed for temporary need, not structural mass unemployment.
- Swiss social sustainability ranks only 17th internationally (SGI 2024) despite high GDP — gaps in childcare, old-age poverty, and gender equity.
- Investment in community mental health infrastructure, peer support networks, and purpose-providing institutions becomes critical when employment can no longer serve this function.

## 3. Purpose & Meaning Beyond Employment

### The identity crisis

When someone asks "What do you do?" they mean your job. In a post-work society, that question has no easy answer. A major psychological hurdle is replacing the sense of purpose that work currently provides.

### Alternative purpose structures

**Volunteering & civic engagement:**
- Community involvement improves mental health, reduces stress, and increases longevity.
- In the age of automation, volunteering remains one of the most meaningful ways to build connection and create purpose.
- But volunteering must be *structured and valued* — informal, unpaid activity alone doesn't replace the social architecture of employment.

**"Virtue Labor" & Social Purpose Markets:**
- Proposal from VKTR: create market incentives for virtuous activities — learning, caregiving, community service, mentoring, creating art, civic participation.
- UBI addresses material needs but ignores the human need for purpose. "Markets With a Social Purpose" would use AI to create incentives for socially valuable non-market activities.

**Post-Work Organizations (PWOs):**
- Scholars propose new institutional structures where participation is voluntary, work is delinked from individual identity, and participatory self-governance (assemblies, councils) replaces hierarchical management.
- Industrial democracy within PWOs could incentivize individuals and provide purpose, even with minimal time commitment.

**Cultural transformation:**
- Society must shift measures of success from professional achievement to contributions to community, creativity, personal growth, and sustainable living.
- This requires deliberate policy: funding arts, community spaces, lifelong learning, and civic institutions.

### The counter-argument
Jean-Philippe Deranty (2022) argues we "cannot and should not wish work away" — human societies are by necessity work societies. Care work, social reproduction, affective labor will always need humans. The question is whether this counts as "employment."

## 4. Lifelong Learning as Social Infrastructure

### The shift from "education-then-work" to continuous learning

The traditional model of front-loaded education followed by a 40-year career is obsolete. Only 0.5% of global GDP is invested in adult lifelong learning; increasing this could boost GDP by $6.5 trillion by 2030.

**Key findings:**
- **WEF Future of Jobs 2025:** 92M jobs displaced by 2030, 170M created — but the displaced workers are not the same people who fill the new roles without massive reskilling.
- **OECD Skills Outlook 2025:** Only 19% of adults without upper-secondary education participate in non-formal learning (vs 61% of tertiary-educated). Learning reaches those who need it least.
- **Skills needed:** Not just technical — critical thinking, creativity, socio-emotional intelligence, AI ethics, human-AI collaboration, adaptability.
- **Micro-credentials:** Growing but issues of trust, recognition, and connection to larger learning trajectories remain unresolved.

### In a high-unemployment society, learning becomes a *purpose activity*:
- Education shifts from being vocational preparation to being a lifelong source of meaning, social connection, and civic participation.
- Switzerland's dual-track VET system (~2/3 of youth) is a strong foundation — but needs extension into mid-career and late-career reskilling.
- WEF Reskilling Revolution: commitments to reach 856M people by 2030.

## 5. Decoupling Basic Services from Employment

### The employment-benefits link as vulnerability

In many countries (especially the U.S.), healthcare, retirement savings, and other benefits are tied to employment. In a high-unemployment future, this link becomes a crisis multiplier.

**Switzerland's advantage:** The Swiss system already partially decouples key services:
- **Healthcare:** Mandatory individual health insurance (KVG) — not employer-linked. Premium subsidies for low-income households.
- **Pensions:** Three-pillar system (AHV/state + BVG/occupational + private). AHV is universal. BVG is employer-linked — vulnerability point.
- **Sozialhilfe:** Cantonal social assistance as last-resort safety net, not tied to employment history.

**Gaps and vulnerabilities:**
- **2nd pillar (BVG):** Employer-linked occupational pension. Mass unemployment would create a massive gap in retirement savings for displaced workers.
- **ALV (unemployment insurance):** Designed for 18 months max. Structural unemployment exceeding this creates a cliff to Sozialhilfe.
- **Sozialhilfe capacity:** Currently costs CHF 2.5 bn/year for ~280,000 recipients. A doubling or tripling of caseload would strain cantonal budgets massively.

**Global direction:**
- World Bank 2025: 2 billion people globally lack adequate social protection. Every $1 transferred to poor families generates $2.50 in local economic activity.
- The trend in progressive policy is toward universal basic services (housing, healthcare, education, transport) provided regardless of employment status.

## 6. Work Sharing & Reduced Working Hours

### Sharing the remaining work

If AI displaces 20–40% of tasks, one response is to distribute remaining work more broadly through shorter hours.

**Key evidence:**
- **Zurich 35-hour trial (2023+):** City of Zurich trialling for shift workers. First Swiss institutional experiment.
- **Four-day week studies:** UK pilot showed maintained productivity with improved wellbeing. Environmental co-benefit: 127M tonnes CO₂ reduction if implemented nationally.
- **Swiss context:** Switzerland already has a 38.7% part-time rate (58% of women). Unusual flexibility exists — but it's gendered and voluntary, not structural work-sharing.
- **Kurzarbeit:** Proven mechanism (COVID: 1.3M workers, CHF 11 bn/year). But designed for temporary shocks, not permanent displacement.

### Degrowth & Job Guarantee frameworks

**Work Time Reduction (WTR):**
- Core degrowth proposal: maintain full employment in a non-growing economy by reducing hours.
- Trade-off: lower individual wages offset by UBI or expanded public services.
- Environmental co-benefit: shorter hours correlate with lower carbon footprint.

**Job Guarantee (JG):**
- Government as employer of last resort for socially useful work (care, environment, community).
- Eliminates the profit constraint on employment — employment becomes a political right.
- Severs the link between aggregate demand and employment, enabling ecological sustainability without unemployment.
- Tension: degrowth scholars debate whether JG conflicts with reducing total economic activity.

## 7. Swiss-Specific Policy Landscape

### What Switzerland already has
| Tool | Status | Readiness for mass unemployment |
|------|--------|-------------------------------|
| ALV (unemployment insurance) | 70–80% replacement, 18 months | ❌ Designed for temporary unemployment |
| Kurzarbeit (short-time work) | Proven at scale (COVID) | ⚠️ Temporary shock only |
| Sozialhilfe (social assistance) | Cantonal, needs-based | ❌ Not scaled for mass caseload |
| AHV (state pension) | Universal, 13th pension from 2026 | ✅ Not employment-dependent |
| KVG (health insurance) | Mandatory individual, with subsidies | ✅ Not employer-linked |
| BVG (occupational pension) | Employer-linked | ❌ Vulnerable to mass unemployment |
| VET (vocational training) | World-class dual-track system | ⚠️ Needs mid/late-career extension |
| Cantonal minimum wages | 5 cantons, CHF 19–24.50/hour | ⚠️ Irrelevant if no jobs exist |

### What Switzerland doesn't have (and may need)
1. **Structural unemployment insurance** — ALV extension or new scheme for permanent displacement beyond 18 months
2. **Universal basic services** — Free/subsidized housing, transport, digital access as rights
3. **Funded civic participation infrastructure** — Paid community roles, structured volunteering with social recognition
4. **BVG reform** — Pension continuity mechanism for permanently displaced workers
5. **Scaled mental health infrastructure** — Community-based, preventive, accessible without employment
6. **Purpose institutions** — Lifelong learning centers, maker spaces, community hubs that provide structure/identity
7. **Social Purpose Market mechanisms** — Incentivizing care, learning, civic participation as valued economic activity

### AI regulation approach
Switzerland has opted for a *lean regulatory approach* — no general AI law like the EU AI Act. Instead: ratification of Council of Europe AI Convention + sector-specific measures. No specific policy linking AI displacement to social safety net reform as of March 2026.

## 8. Democratic Legitimacy Under Mass Unemployment

### The political threat

Unemployment doesn't just destroy livelihoods — it destroys faith in democratic institutions. This is one of the clearest lessons of the 20th century.

**Historical evidence:**
- **Weimar Germany:** Mass unemployment was the springboard for Hitler's rise. "No work, no peace."
- **Greece 2010s:** 58% youth unemployment → collapse of establishment parties, rise of Syriza (far-left) and Golden Dawn (neo-Nazi), mass street protests, brain drain emigration.
- **Spain 2010s:** 55% youth unemployment → 18% widening of income distribution (largest in Europe), loss of purchasing power of 25.7% for under-35s.
- **Global 2025:** International IDEA issued 20 democratic alerts in just 4 months of 2025 — twice the rate of any previous full year.

**The mechanism:** Economic insecurity → loss of faith in mainstream parties → populist mobilization of marginalized groups → erosion of democratic checks and balances. The V-Dem Democracy Report identified 24 cases of stand-alone autocratization and 20 bell-turn cases in 2025.

**The populism paradox:** Populism can re-engage disaffected voters (a democratic good) while simultaneously undermining institutional safeguards (a democratic threat). All forms — even progressive ones — "harbor an intrinsic tendency to become a runaway train."

**Institutional trust collapse:** UK data shows government trusted by only 27% of citizens, parliament at 24%, parties at 12%. Distrust of MPs rose from 54% (2014) to 76% (2024).

### Policy implications for high-unemployment society:
- **Direct democracy as buffer:** Switzerland's direct democracy may be uniquely resilient — citizens *are* the government, reducing the "us vs. them" dynamic that fuels populism.
- **Economic participation beyond employment:** If people can't participate economically through jobs, they need other forms of meaningful participation (civic roles, community governance, direct democratic engagement).
- **Inclusive institutions:** Maintaining democratic legitimacy requires that unemployed citizens feel they have genuine voice and agency, not just subsistence.

## 9. Physical Infrastructure for Abundant Free Time

### The built environment for a post-work society

If millions of people have vastly more free time, what physical spaces do they need? Current urban design assumes people spend 8+ hours at workplaces. A high-unemployment society needs different infrastructure.

**Libraries as the model:**
- Libraries are already evolving into the community hubs a post-work society needs: free, open to all regardless of income or status, offering maker spaces, media production, WiFi, social connection, and creative resources.
- "Literally anyone can come into our public library. You do not have to buy anything. You do not even have to read a book." — Brooklyn Public Library
- Eric Klinenberg (*Palaces for the People*): libraries have the greatest potential for connecting people and fighting inequality, polarization, and civic decline.

**Maker spaces:**
- Democratize access to 3D printers, laser cutters, design software — tools previously limited to manufacturers.
- Foster creativity, problem-solving, and community. Transformative for underserved communities.
- Libraries + maker spaces = the nucleus of post-work creative infrastructure.

**Public spaces and urban planning:**
- Parks, plazas, community gardens, sports facilities become essential infrastructure (not luxuries) when people have 40+ extra hours per week.
- Research identifies 7 pathways linking urban planning to subjective wellbeing: travel, leisure, work, social relationships, residential wellbeing, emotional responses, and health.
- Social infrastructure — laundrettes, swimming pools, playgrounds, community centers — creates the "accidental encounters" that build social cohesion.

**The "atrophied muscle" problem:**
- Research shows that currently, away from work, people mainly "watch TV, try to sleep, and in general vegetate." Post-work theorists argue this reflects how work culture has atrophied our capacity for self-directed activity.
- Investment in structured but voluntary activity spaces (workshops, studios, sports clubs, learning centers) helps people rebuild this capacity.

## 10. Historical Lessons: Societies That Lived With High Unemployment

### What actually happened when unemployment hit 25–55%?

**The Great Depression (1929–1939):**
- U.S. peak: 24.9% unemployment (1933), 12.8M people. Wage income fell 42.5%.
- Social devastation: bank failures wiped savings, "Hoovervilles" (shanty towns), families split, youth gangs riding boxcars.
- **Political divergence:** Same economic crisis → New Deal reforms (U.S.), centrist coalition (UK), fascist tyranny (Germany). Institutional resilience and leadership determined outcomes.
- **What worked:** Social Security, unemployment insurance, public works (CCC, WPA). The New Deal created a fundamentally new relationship between citizens and government.
- **What didn't work:** The Depression only truly ended with WWII military spending — pure civilian policy was insufficient at this scale.

**Greece (2010–2018):**
- Youth unemployment peaked at 58.1% (2012). Overall unemployment above 25%.
- Austerity measures *worsened* the crisis — described as "the economic massacre of the young generation."
- Political consequences: collapse of establishment parties, rise of Syriza, Golden Dawn, mass emigration of skilled youth.
- Over-qualification crisis: 26% of workers over-qualified for their jobs, universities trained for jobs that didn't exist.

**Spain (2008–2018):**
- Youth unemployment peaked at 55% (2012). Workers under 35 lost 25.7% purchasing power.
- Widest expansion of income inequality in Europe (18% increase).
- Youth Guarantee scheme (2013) was poorly implemented — many eligible youth never heard of it.
- Dual labor market (permanent vs. temporary contracts) created structural insider/outsider divide.

### Key lessons:
1. **Aggregate demand matters most** — cutting spending during high unemployment deepens the crisis (Greece, 1937 U.S. recession-within-depression).
2. **Safety nets prevent radicalization** — Social Security, UI, and active labor market programs maintain social cohesion.
3. **Institutional quality determines political outcomes** — same economic shock, very different trajectories depending on democratic resilience.
4. **Youth are the canary** — youth unemployment has the worst scarring effects and the strongest link to political instability.
5. **Austerity is counterproductive** — every historical case shows that cutting spending during mass unemployment makes it worse.
6. **Generic reforms fail** — targeted, specific policies (school-to-work transitions, early intervention, Austrian "training guarantee") outperform broad structural reforms.

## 11. Swiss-Specific Assets: Direct Democracy, Vereine & Militia System

### Why Switzerland may be uniquely resilient

Switzerland has three cultural-institutional assets that could serve as foundations for a high-unemployment society — if deliberately strengthened.

**The Militia System (Milizsystem):**
- Central pillar of Swiss identity alongside direct democracy and federalism.
- Citizens assume public responsibilities (fire brigades, lay judges, school boards, parliament) with minimal compensation.
- Rooted in the Federal Charter of 1291; constitutionally enshrined (Art. 58) since 1999.
- The principle: financial independence from public office → more independent decision-making → less bureaucracy.
- **Relevance to high unemployment:** The militia system already assumes that citizens contribute to governance *alongside* their jobs. In a post-work society, it could become the primary structure for civic engagement — expanded from a part-time addition to a core life activity.

**Vereine (Associations):**
- Switzerland has ~90,000 associations. 41% of the population participates in formal volunteer work; 51% do informal volunteering.
- Vereine are the social glue: people who volunteer have higher trust in fellow citizens and political institutions, feel more connected to their community, and are more likely to participate in direct democracy.
- 55% of formal volunteers reject the statement "I have no say in what the government does" vs. only 36% of non-volunteers.
- **Relevance:** Vereine already provide purpose, social connection, and community identity outside employment. They are the existing infrastructure that a post-work society could scale up.

**Direct Democracy:**
- Citizens don't see government as "the enemy" but as democracy *by and for* citizens.
- This reduces the "us vs. them" dynamic that fuels populism in representative democracies when unemployment rises.
- Referendums, initiatives, and cantonal Landsgemeinden give even economically excluded citizens a direct voice.
- **Relevance:** Direct democracy may be Switzerland's strongest buffer against the democratic legitimacy crisis that mass unemployment causes elsewhere (see Section 8).

**The challenge:**
- The militia system is under pressure: work-life demands make part-time civic office increasingly burdensome.
- If volunteering doesn't increase, social cohesion "will crumble in the long term."
- Paradoxically, mass unemployment could *help* — more free time means more capacity for civic engagement, *if* the infrastructure and incentives exist.

## Summary: A Toolkit for Living With High Unemployment

The paradigm shift is: **stop trying only to keep people employed — start building the infrastructure for people to live well without traditional employment.**

| Domain | Tool | Function |
|--------|------|----------|
| **Income** | UBI / Guaranteed Income | Material floor regardless of employment |
| **Income** | Sovereign Wealth Fund / AI dividends | Public ownership of automated economy's returns |
| **Income** | AI/automation taxation | Fund social infrastructure from productivity gains |
| **Income** | Universal Basic Services | Free healthcare, education, housing, transport |
| **Health** | Universal healthcare (CH: already exists) | Decouple health from employment |
| **Mental health** | Community mental health infrastructure | Address identity/purpose crisis at scale |
| **Purpose** | Social Purpose Markets / "Virtue Labor" | Incentivize care, learning, civic work |
| **Purpose** | Expanded Militia System / civic roles | Scale Switzerland's existing civic participation |
| **Purpose** | Lifelong learning as social institution | Education as meaning, not just vocational |
| **Purpose** | Strengthened Vereine (associations) | Scale existing social infrastructure |
| **Housing** | Universal basic housing / expanded social housing | Decouple shelter from income |
| **Pensions** | BVG reform / universal pension expansion | Close employer-linked gap |
| **Work sharing** | Reduced hours / 4-day week | Distribute remaining work broadly |
| **Work sharing** | Job Guarantee | Government as employer of last resort |
| **Physical infra** | Libraries, maker spaces, community hubs | Built environment for abundant free time |
| **Physical infra** | Parks, plazas, sports, community gardens | Public spaces as essential (not luxury) infrastructure |
| **Democracy** | Direct democracy (CH: already exists) | Maintain legitimacy when jobs can't |
| **Democracy** | Expanded civic participation rights | Give economically excluded citizens voice and agency |
| **Social cohesion** | Community hubs, local institutions | Build belonging outside workplace |
| **Identity** | Cultural shift in success metrics | Redefine value beyond professional achievement |

### The central question for the simulator

The existing labourshift tool models a consumption-collapse spiral and tests counter-measures that try to reduce unemployment. The new direction would model **societal resilience infrastructure**: what investments in housing, healthcare, community, income support, and democratic participation are needed to maintain quality of life and social stability at various unemployment levels (10%, 15%, 20%, 25%+)?

Key modelling variables:
- **Social cohesion index** as a function of: income floor, community infrastructure investment, civic participation rates, mental health service capacity
- **Democratic stability index** as a function of: unemployment rate, income inequality (Gini), institutional trust, direct democratic participation
- **Wellbeing index** as a function of: purpose activities (volunteering, learning, care), physical infrastructure, social connection, health access
- **Fiscal sustainability** as a function of: AI-derived revenue (SWF dividends, automation tax), reduced traditional tax base, social spending requirements
