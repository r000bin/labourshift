# Living With High Unemployment: Tools for a Post-Work Society

Research compiled March 2026 — focusing not on preventing unemployment, but on the social infrastructure needed when a large fraction of the population is permanently displaced from traditional employment.

---

## 1. Income Security Without Employment

### Universal Basic Income (UBI) & Guaranteed Income (GI)

**Current state:** No country has implemented nationwide UBI as of 2026, but 160+ pilots have been conducted globally (Stanford Basic Income Lab). Most real-world programs are technically Guaranteed Basic Income (GBI) — means-tested, phased out as income rises.

**Key evidence:**
- **Swiss 2016 referendum:** CHF 2,500/month UBI rejected 76.9% to 23.1%. Federal Council estimated gross cost CHF 208 bn/year, net CHF 25 bn after replacing existing transfers. This remains the political baseline.
- **Finland KELA experiment (2017–2018):** 2,000 randomly selected unemployed persons received €560/month unconditionally. Results: participants were happier, healthier, and more trusting of institutions. Employment effect was modest — 6 more days worked per year — but participants who *did* work earned more than controls (€21 vs €16/day). The trial was not extended due to political opposition and was superseded by Finland's "activation model."
- **NBER 2024 study (ORUS — OpenResearch Unconditional Cash Study):** $1,000/month payments to 1,000 low-income Americans for 3 years. Reduced labor supply by 1.3–1.4 hours/week, 3.9pp less likely to be employed. Decline was on the *extensive margin* (people leaving work entirely). Recipients spent freed time on leisure, not job search or training. Total household income increased only $500/month (not $1,000) due to offsetting reduced earnings.
- **Cambridge (Jaimovich et al.):** UBI at 10% of average income + existing UI could *increase* output 1.27% and reduce unemployment 0.5pp. Income elasticity of labor force participation: −0.1 to −0.3.
- **GiveDirectly Kenya (ongoing, largest global trial):** $0.75/day (~$22.50/month) to 20,000+ villagers in rural Kenya. Positive effects on consumption, assets, food security, and psychological wellbeing, but mixed evidence on productive investment.
- **Wales pilot (2022–2026):** £1,600/month for care leavers turning 18. Mid-trial: improved mental health, more balanced lives, pursuit of education.
- **Stockton SEED (2019–2021):** $500/month for 125 residents. Full-time employment *increased* from 28% to 40% (vs 32% to 37% in control). Reduced income volatility, improved mental health, and increased goal-setting.
- **U.S. Guaranteed Income Pilot Program Act 2025:** Proposed 3-year pilot with monthly payments equal to local fair market rent for 2-bedroom home. $495M authorized for FY2026–2030.

**The labor supply tension:** UBI consistently reduces labor force participation in experiments. But in a high-unemployment future, this may be a *feature* not a bug — fewer people competing for fewer jobs, with income floor preventing destitution. The ORUS finding that reduced work hours went to leisure (not civic participation or education) is a warning: without complementary purpose infrastructure, UBI alone may not produce flourishing.

**Fiscal reality for Switzerland:** At CHF 2,500/month for ~7M adults = ~CHF 210 bn gross. Current total social expenditure is CHF 179.5 bn. Net cost after replacing existing transfers is the key figure — estimated at CHF 25 bn (2016 estimate), roughly 3% of GDP. The debt brake (Article 126 BV, approved by 85% of voters in 2001) severely constrains this: the Confederation may only spend as much as it collects, adjusted for the economic cycle. Any sustained UBI program would require either extraordinary budget approval (absolute majority in both chambers, 6-year payback) or a constitutional amendment via mandatory referendum (double majority).

### Beyond UBI: Sovereign Wealth Funds, AI Taxation & Universal Basic Services

UBI is only one funding mechanism. Several complementary approaches are being developed:

**Sovereign Wealth Funds (SWFs) for AI dividends:**
- Convergence Analysis (2025) proposes SWFs with three roles: **Lead** (invest in frontier AI), **Own** (acquire equity in AI companies for public benefit), **Share** (distribute returns as universal basic dividends).
- SWFs can also serve as stabilization funds — smoothing tax revenues and labor shocks from rapid automation.
- U.S. Executive Order (Feb 2025): President Trump signed an order directing creation of a U.S. sovereign wealth fund "for the sole benefit of American citizens."
- SWFs invested $46 bn in AI ventures in the first 8 months of 2025 alone.
- **Norway's GPFG** (Government Pension Fund Global): $1.7 trillion, returns roughly $180 bn/year. Funded by petroleum revenues. Demonstrates that a well-managed SWF can sustain significant citizen dividends.
- **Alaska Permanent Fund:** Distributes annual dividends to every Alaska resident (typically $1,000–$3,000/year). Funded by oil revenues. Shows political durability — popular and difficult to reduce.
- **Swiss context:** Switzerland lacks oil/gas wealth but could capitalize a SWF through AI-specific levies. The SNB's balance sheet (~120% of GDP, ~CHF 1 trillion at peak in 2021–2022) already functions as a quasi-SWF — but its mandate is price stability, not redistribution.

**Sam Altman's "American Equity Fund":**
- Large AI companies and landholders contribute ~2.5% of their value annually to a fund distributed to all citizens — effectively public ownership of the automated economy.

**Data royalty models:**
- Treat public data as a natural resource. AI companies pay royalties for training data, proceeds distributed via SWF.

**AI/Automation taxation:**
- Consumption taxes and land value taxes are most promising in an automated economy (purchases still happen even without human labor).
- Direct "robot taxes" face definitional challenges — what counts as "automation" is hard to draw a line around.
- MIT research (Costinot & Werning 2022): optimal robot tax should be "modest" to avoid distortions. Swiss-specific constraint: FDI elasticity and cantonal tax competition make this the least viable Swiss option.
- **OECD/G20 minimum corporate tax:** Swiss voters approved implementation in June 2023. Supplementary tax took effect January 2024 — 75% of revenue to cantons/communes, 25% to Confederation. Partially addresses base erosion but not AI-specific.
- **EU AI Act levies (external constraint):** Switzerland has not adopted the EU AI Act but must maintain compatibility with its bilateral path. Any Swiss AI tax must avoid triggering EU retaliatory measures.

**Universal Basic Services (UBS):**
- Alternative to cash transfers: provide free/subsidized healthcare, education, housing, transport, digital access.
- **IGP (UCL Institute for Global Prosperity) proposal:** UBS covering housing, food, transport, information access — costed at ~2.3% of UK GDP. Applied to Switzerland (GDP ~CHF 800 bn): roughly CHF 18.4 bn/year.
- Hybrid models combining modest cash (UBI) + universal services may be more politically viable and fiscally efficient than pure UBI.
- **Swiss advantage:** Healthcare (KVG) is already individually mandated and not employer-linked. The infrastructure for UBS expansion exists — the question is funding and political will.

**Fiscal risk:** If the "AI bubble" bursts (analysts warned by late 2025 that AI revenue hasn't matched investment hype), funding sources for equity-based redistribution could evaporate. Diversification of funding mechanisms is critical.

## 2. Mental Health & Social Cohesion Infrastructure

### The psychological crisis of mass unemployment

Work provides far more than income — it structures time, confers identity, creates social bonds, and gives purpose. Loss of employment correlates with depression, anxiety, low self-esteem, and stress-related illness across all social strata.

### The Jahoda Framework: What Employment Actually Provides

**Marie Jahoda's Latent Deprivation Theory** (*Employment and Unemployment: A Social-Psychological Analysis*, 1982) remains the foundational framework. Drawing from her landmark *Marienthal* study (1933) — an ethnography of an Austrian village where the textile mill closed, leaving near-total unemployment — Jahoda identified five *latent functions* of employment beyond income:

1. **Time structure** — imposing a temporal framework on the day
2. **Social contact** — providing regular interaction with people outside the nuclear family
3. **Collective purpose** — linking individual activity to larger societal goals
4. **Status and identity** — defining one's social position
5. **Enforced activity** — compelling regular engagement and effort

The *Marienthal* finding was devastating: unemployment did not radicalize the villagers but instead produced **resignation, apathy, and the collapse of time discipline**. Library usage *fell* despite unlimited free time. Social and political participation withered. The community turned inward, relationships frayed, and a pervasive sense of purposelessness took hold.

**Post-Jahoda evidence:**
- **Paul & Moser (2009) meta-analysis** (237 cross-sectional, 87 longitudinal studies): Unemployed individuals had **significantly worse** mental health (d = 0.51, a medium-to-large effect size) across distress, depression, anxiety, psychosomatic symptoms, subjective wellbeing, and self-esteem. Moderators: the effect was *stronger* in countries with weaker unemployment protection and higher income inequality.
- **2025 systematic review:** Re-employment reverses most mental health effects, confirming the causal direction. The effect operates *independently* of income loss.
- **Dose-response relationship:** Longer unemployment duration progressively worsens mental health. The effect is cumulative and non-linear — the first 6 months are the most damaging, with continued but decelerating decline thereafter.

### Deindustrialisation Evidence: What Happens to Communities

**UK coalfield communities (post-1985):**
- The most comprehensively studied case of regional deindustrialisation. After the miners' strike defeat (1984–85) and subsequent pit closures, former coalfield communities experienced elevated mortality, particularly from cardiovascular disease and suicide, persisting *decades* after closures.
- "Deaths of despair" — a term coined by Case & Deaton (2015) for the U.S. but equally applicable to UK coalfields — captures the intertwined rise of addiction, suicide, and chronic disease in communities stripped of their economic purpose.
- The Marmot Review (2010, *Fair Society, Healthy Lives*) documented the gradient: each step down the social hierarchy produced worse health outcomes, driven by loss of control, social isolation, and status anxiety — not just poverty per se.

**U.S. "China Shock" communities (Autor, Dorn, Hanson):**
- Manufacturing job losses produced not just unemployment but *spillover effects*: reduced marriage and fertility rates, increased male idleness and premature mortality, rising share of single-parent households.
- Displaced workers did NOT migrate to new regions as economic theory predicted — they stayed and suffered. Effects persisted two decades beyond the initial shock.

**Swiss Watch Crisis communities (Jura, Neuchâtel, 1970s–80s):**
- Employment collapsed from 90,000 to 28,000 (1970–1988) — a two-thirds reduction.
- Geographic concentration of impact in the Jura arc. Recovery came through luxury repositioning (Swatch Group, Nicolas Hayek) but the human cost was enormous and concentrated.

### Swiss Mental Health Data

- **Swiss Health Survey (2022):** 19% of the Swiss population reported high psychological distress. Among the unemployed, rates were significantly higher. Women reported higher psychological distress than men across all employment categories.
- **Psychiatrists per capita:** Switzerland has one of the highest ratios in the OECD (~50 per 100,000 population), but access varies enormously by canton. Urban cantons (Zurich, Basel, Geneva) have 3–5x the density of rural cantons (Appenzell, Uri).
- **Mental health spending:** Switzerland spends approximately CHF 11 billion/year on mental health services (~6% of total health expenditure). However, outpatient psychotherapy was only integrated into mandatory health insurance (KVG) from July 2022 — the "Anordnungsmodell" reform.
- **Waiting times:** Average 3–6 months for psychotherapy in German-speaking Switzerland; longer in French and Italian-speaking regions.

### Social Cohesion: The Critical Buffer

- **Neighbourhood social cohesion** has a *greater impact* on mental health than economic deprivation alone (SSPH+ 2025). Trust, belonging, and feeling valued are the strongest predictors of wellbeing.
- **EU policy direction:** Europe is working toward a binding directive on Mental Health and Work (target: ~2029), recognizing that mass alienation feeds extremism and populism.
- **The "status syndrome" (Michael Marmot):** Psychological experience of inequality and lack of control over one's life drives mental health outcomes more than absolute deprivation. The Whitehall Studies showed that civil servants one grade below the top had *worse* health than those at the top, despite adequate income — demonstrating that relative status, not absolute poverty, is the key driver.

### Swiss Social Cohesion Indicators

- **Vereine participation:** ~100,000 voluntary associations; ~55% of residents aged 16+ participate in at least one Verein. Sports clubs alone: 18,440 clubs with ~2.5 million active members under Swiss Olympic.
- **Social trust:** Switzerland consistently ranks among the top 5 OECD countries for interpersonal trust. The 2024 Edelman Trust Barometer placed Swiss institutional trust well above the global median.
- **Volunteering rates:** 41% formal volunteering, 51% informal volunteering. Volunteers have higher trust in institutions and are more likely to participate in direct democracy.
- **Risk factor:** The militia system is under strain — willingness to serve has declined throughout the 21st century due to greater individualism, mobility, and demanding work environments. Gender gap: 1.7% of men vs. 0.5% of women volunteer in political roles (2020).

### Policy implications for Switzerland:
- Switzerland's cantonal Sozialhilfe system guarantees a "dignified existence" but is designed for temporary need, not structural mass unemployment.
- Swiss social sustainability ranks only 17th internationally (SGI 2024) despite high GDP — gaps in childcare, old-age poverty, and gender equity.
- Investment in community mental health infrastructure, peer support networks, and purpose-providing institutions becomes critical when employment can no longer serve Jahoda's five latent functions.
- **The Vereine infrastructure is the primary non-work source of social cohesion** — but it is under demographic and cultural pressure. Strengthening Vereine may be the single most cost-effective intervention for maintaining social cohesion in a post-work transition.
- **Paradox of displacement:** AI displacement could *revitalise* the militia system and Vereine — the current crisis stems partly from overworked professionals lacking time. If 20–40% of the workforce has substantially more free time, this bottleneck eases.

## 3. Purpose & Meaning Beyond Employment

### The Philosophical Foundations

**Hannah Arendt's *Vita Activa*** (*The Human Condition*, 1958) distinguishes three fundamental human activities:
- **Labour:** Biological necessity — cyclical activities to sustain life. Shared with animals; nothing distinctively human.
- **Work:** World-building — producing durable objects (buildings, books, institutions) that outlast the maker.
- **Action:** The highest tier — inserting something "original and unanticipated" into the public sphere through speech and deeds. Where humans disclose *who* they are; where political freedom resides.

Arendt's warning is directly relevant: modernity has collapsed these distinctions, reducing all activity to mere *labour* (production for consumption). If AI eliminates labour and much of work, the crucial question is whether society can cultivate conditions for *action* — meaningful civic and political engagement — rather than freeing people into purposeless consumption. She warned that "the loser in all these shifts... is action."

**Aristotle's *Eudaimonia*:** Flourishing is "an activity of soul in accordance with virtue in a complete life" (*Nicomachean Ethics*, I.7). Leisure (*schole*) is a prerequisite — but sharply distinct from idleness. Leisure is time for self-development, contemplation, and civic engagement. For post-work policy: freed time must be structured around virtue-developing activities, not merely entertainment.

**John Stuart Mill's Higher Pleasures:** Qualitative hedonism distinguishes lower (bodily) from higher (intellectual, aesthetic, moral) pleasures. "It is better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied." Policy implication: a post-work society must invest in *conditions* for higher pleasures (education, culture, civic life) rather than assume material provision alone produces wellbeing.

### Psychology of Meaning

**Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan):** Three basic psychological needs essential for intrinsic motivation and wellbeing:
1. **Autonomy** — feeling volitional, endorsing one's own behaviour
2. **Competence** — experiencing mastery and effectiveness
3. **Relatedness** — connection and belonging with others

Martela, Lehmus-Sun, Parker, Pessi & Ryan (2023) confirmed across 27 European countries that these needs are closely connected with wellbeing and meaning. For post-work policy: any replacement for employment must satisfy all three needs. Volunteer work, civic participation, and structured learning are strong candidates; passive consumption is not.

**Csikszentmihalyi's Flow:** Optimal experience occurs when skill level matches challenge, producing intense concentration, loss of self-awareness, and intrinsic reward. Post-work design requires *challenging, skill-matched activities*, not just free time.

**Seligman's PERMA Model** (*Flourish*, 2011) — five elements of wellbeing:
1. **Positive Emotion** — hedonic happiness
2. **Engagement** — flow states
3. **Relationships** — social bonds
4. **Meaning** — serving something "bigger than the self"
5. **Accomplishment** — achieving goals for their own sake

Currently, employment bundles several PERMA elements. Post-work institutions must *unbundle and reprovide* these through alternative structures.

### Post-Work Theorists

- **Andre Gorz** (*Reclaiming Work*, 1999): Wage-based society is historically contingent. Liberation from work requires new forms of self-determined activity. Foundational to the post-work tradition.
- **Kathi Weeks** (*The Problem with Work*, 2011): Feminist-Marxist critique — the work ethic is a political construct, not a natural drive. Advocates UBI and reduced hours as feminist demands.
- **David Graeber** (*Bullshit Jobs*, 2018): 20–50% of jobs in affluent countries may be pointless. YouGov (UK, 2015): 37% said their jobs don't contribute meaningfully. Cambridge/Birmingham study (2021): only 4.8% felt they "rarely" do useful work. Simon Walo (University of Zurich, 2023) — first quantitative study — found 19% of American respondents said they "never" or "rarely" had positive impact.
- **Nick Srnicek & Alex Williams** (*Inventing the Future*, 2015): Four-part program — full automation, reduced working week, UBI, cultural shift away from work ethic. Argue the left must pursue long-term institutional strategies modeled on neoliberalism's Mont Pelerin approach.

### Evidence from Retirees, Sabbaticals, and Lottery Winners

**Lottery winners:**
- Kuhn et al. (2011, *American Economic Review*): Dutch Postcode Lottery winners were *not happier* 6 months after winning. Effects confined to durable goods.
- Brickman et al. (1978): Lottery winners not happier than controls and took *less* pleasure from mundane events.
- Nearly **two-thirds of lottery winners continue working full-time**, suggesting work provides something beyond income.
- Lindqvist, Östling & Cesarini (2018, *Review of Economic Studies*): Swedish lottery — modest positive long-run life satisfaction effects, but smaller than expected.

**Retirees:**
- Yemiscigil, Powdthavee & Whillans (2021, *Psychological Science*, N=8,113): Sizable *increase* in purpose in life post-retirement — but driven primarily by those with low SES retiring from dissatisfying jobs. For those with meaningful work, retirement can *decrease* purpose.

### Alternative Purpose Structures

**Institutions that satisfy SDT + enable Flow + provide PERMA:**
- **Care economy:** Elder care, childcare, community health — satisfies relatedness and meaning.
- **Arts and cultural production:** Enables flow, engagement, accomplishment.
- **Civic service:** Provides meaning ("bigger than self"), relatedness, competence.
- **Open-source contribution:** Autonomy, competence, global relatedness. Switzerland hosts key open-source infrastructure (CERN, various foundations).
- **Environmental stewardship:** Aligns with meaning/purpose; Switzerland's strong environmental consciousness.

**"Virtue Labor" & Social Purpose Markets:**
- Proposal from VKTR: create market incentives for virtuous activities — learning, caregiving, community service, mentoring, creating art, civic participation.
- UBI addresses material needs but ignores the human need for purpose. "Markets With a Social Purpose" would use AI to create incentives for socially valuable non-market activities.

**Post-Work Organizations (PWOs):**
- Scholars propose new institutional structures where participation is voluntary, work is delinked from individual identity, and participatory self-governance (assemblies, councils) replaces hierarchical management.

### Swiss Context: The Zwingli/Calvin Heritage as Asset

Switzerland's Protestant Reformation was uniquely civic-oriented:
- **Huldrych Zwingli** (Zurich, from 1519): Viewed the Reformation as simultaneously religious, political, and social. Created effective Poor Law systems. Resigned his episcopal appointment and received a *civic* commission instead.
- **John Calvin** (Geneva, from 1536): Strict moral discipline integrating religious doctrine into city governance. Predestination encouraged worldly success as a sign of election — the Protestant work ethic.
- This produced a deeply **civic-oriented** culture intertwining religious discipline, community participation, and moral accountability with political self-governance.

**The dual heritage — work ethic *and* civic duty — is an asset for post-work transition.** The Swiss tradition already contains robust institutions of community engagement beyond employment. The concept of *Eigenverantwortung* (personal responsibility, Article 6 BV) would need to evolve from "supporting yourself through work" toward "contributing to community through diverse channels."

### The Swiss Zivildienst as Model

**Current system:** Alternative civilian service for male conscientious objectors — social work, environmental protection, agriculture, public administration. In 2024: 6,799 newly admitted; record service days (51.6% social services, 16.6% schools, 14.8% healthcare, 9.7% environmental).

**November 2025 Civic Duty Initiative:** Proposed extending compulsory service to all citizens including women. **Rejected overwhelmingly (84% against).** But the existing framework could be *voluntarily expanded* — structured civic engagement with stipends, without compulsion.

### The counter-argument
Jean-Philippe Deranty (2022) argues we "cannot and should not wish work away" — human societies are by necessity work societies. Care work, social reproduction, affective labor will always need humans. The question is whether this counts as "employment."

## 4. Lifelong Learning as Social Infrastructure

### The shift from "education-then-work" to continuous learning

The traditional model of front-loaded education followed by a 40-year career is obsolete. Only 0.5% of global GDP is invested in adult lifelong learning; increasing this could boost GDP by $6.5 trillion by 2030.

**Key findings:**
- **WEF Future of Jobs 2025:** 92M jobs displaced by 2030, 170M created — but the displaced workers are not the same people who fill the new roles without massive reskilling.
- **OECD Skills Outlook 2025:** Only 19% of adults without upper-secondary education participate in non-formal learning (vs 61% of tertiary-educated). Learning reaches those who need it least.
- **Skills needed:** Not just technical — critical thinking, creativity, socio-emotional intelligence, AI ethics, human-AI collaboration, adaptability.
- **Micro-credentials:** Growing but issues of trust, recognition, and connection to larger learning trajectories remain unresolved.

### Switzerland's VET System: World-Class but Occupation-Dependent

Switzerland's dual-track VET system is globally preeminent:
- **~2/3 of youth** opt for vocational training after compulsory education, choosing from **250 different occupations**.
- **~90% of upper-secondary VET students** are in apprenticeships (3–4 days/week workplace + 1–2 days/week classroom).
- **~30% of all Swiss companies** participate in training VET students.
- Two formats: EBA (2-year Federal VET Certificate) and EFZ (3–4 year Federal VET Diploma).
- Directly credited with Switzerland's very low youth unemployment rate.
- Switzerland topped the **IMD World Competitiveness Ranking 2025** (#1 overall), **World Talent Ranking 2025**, **Global Innovation Index 2025** (WIPO), and **Eight Competitiveness Report 2025**.

**The challenge: occupational disappearance.** The VET system is *occupationally organized* — the 250 apprenticeship profiles correspond to specific job roles. If AI eliminates or transforms entire categories, the question is whether VET's architecture can pivot from job-specific training to broader capability development. The contrast between *Bildung* (self-cultivation) and *Ausbildung* (vocational training) becomes critical.

### Swiss Lifelong Learning Participation

- **67.5%** of people aged 25–65 participated in continuing education and training (2016) — well above the **EU average of 42%**.
- **COVID impact:** Participation dropped to **45%** in 2021 (a ~17pp decrease).
- **OECD PIAAC 2023:** 71% of Swiss adults with high literacy (Level 4+) participated in formal/non-formal education in the past year, vs. only **24%** at Level 1 or below — a stark **skills gap in access**.
- Total annual spending on continuing education: **CHF 5.3 billion/year**, largely privately financed.
- **Key inequality:** Participation correlates strongly with existing qualification level, employment status, age, and migration status. Higher-educated adults participate far more than lower-educated.

### WeBiG (Federal Act on Continuing Education, 2017)

First national law regulating non-formal continuing education. Core principle: "Each individual bears responsibility for his or her continuing education," but employers and government share co-responsibility. Article 16 provides federal assistance to cantons for basic competences. **Limitation:** WeBiG is a framework law, not a funding mechanism. Swiss adult education remains largely privately financed.

| Uptake | Near-universal | >1 in 2 eligible Singaporeans aged 30–75 | High but fraud concerns |

**Danish Flexicurity ("Golden Triangle"):** Easy hiring/firing + generous unemployment benefits (up to 2 years at 70–80%) + mandatory active labour market programs. Denmark spends **more on ALMPs than any other OECD country**. Workers accept economic change because they trust the safety net. Challenge: expensive (high taxation); marginal part-time work has doubled since 2000.

**Singapore SkillsFuture (2015):** Every citizen 25+ receives S$500 credit. Citizens 40+ get additional S$4,000 Mid-Career credit. From May 2025: training allowance of 50% income, capped at S$3,000/month, for up to 24 months. First year: >36,000 users, >S$24M in claims.

**France CPF:** Every worker accumulates EUR 500/year (EUR 800 for lower-qualified), up to EUR 5,000 cap (EUR 8,000). Portable across employers, persists through unemployment.

### The Case for *Bildung*

*Bildung* — the German tradition of self-cultivation — offers a philosophical alternative to purely vocational training:
- Originates in 13th-century mysticism, developed by Herder, Humboldt, Goethe, Schiller.
- Emphasizes holistic personal development: "a lifelong process of human development, rather than mere training."
- Humboldt conceived *Bildung* as self-improvement as an end in itself, "independent of any utilitarian or pragmatic reason."
- **AI-era relevance:** A 2026 study (*Bildung at a Crossroads: Teacher Education in the Age of GenAI*) argued GenAI makes Bildung *more* relevant — technical skills are automatable while self-formation, critical judgment, and moral reasoning are not.

### Swiss University Continuing Education Capacity

- **ETH Zurich** (School for Continuing Education): MAS (60+ ECTS), DAS (30+ ECTS), CAS (10+ ECTS). Requires Master's degree. Block courses for part-time study.
- **EPFL** (Extension School): Cybersecurity, AI (including ethics/law), 3D printing, advanced manufacturing, personalized healthcare, green transition. From 2025: cap of 3,000 first-year students due to capacity constraints.
- **Tax deductions** available for professional continuing education costs. Workers with upper-secondary diploma can deduct training costs from taxable income.

### Cost Estimates for a Swiss Lifelong Learning Entitlement

- Current spending: CHF 5.3 bn/year (largely private).
- Swiss working-age population (25–64): ~5.2 million.
- **Singapore-style credit** (S$500/~CHF 340 per person/year): ~CHF 1.8 bn/year.
- **Denmark-style system** with income replacement during retraining (~1.3% of GDP): ~CHF 10.4 bn/year.
- The incremental cost depends on ambition: from modest credits (CHF 2 bn) to comprehensive retraining with income support (CHF 10+ bn).

### In a high-unemployment society, learning becomes a *purpose activity*:
- Education shifts from being vocational preparation to being a lifelong source of meaning, social connection, and civic participation — satisfying SDT's autonomy, competence, and relatedness needs.
- WEF Reskilling Revolution: commitments to reach 856M people by 2030.

## 5. Decoupling Basic Services from Employment

### Services Currently Tied to Employment

| Service | Employment Link | Risk if Employment Shrinks |
|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| **Health Insurance (KVG)** | **Individual** (not employer-linked) | Low — Swiss structural advantage |
| **AHV/OASI (1st pillar pension)** | Contributions salary-based (5.3% each employer/employee); pay-as-you-go | High — fewer workers = lower contribution base |
| **BVG/LPP (2nd pillar pension)** | Fully employer-linked; mandatory for employees earning >CHF 22,680/yr | **Critical** — entire system collapses without employment |
| **UVG (Accident Insurance)** | **Employer-arranged and employer-paid** (occupational) | High — coverage ceases when employment ends (31-day grace) |
| **Daily Allowance Insurance** | Employer-arranged; compensates salary during illness | High — no employment = no coverage |
| **ALV (Unemployment Insurance)** | Contribution-based (employed); benefits 70–80% of salary | High — assumes re-employment is the goal |
| **Family Allowances** | Paid via employer alongside salary | Moderate — could be restructured |

### The BVG (Second Pillar) Problem — The Most Acute Vulnerability

**Scale:** Total BVG assets at end of 2023: **CHF 1,127.9 billion** (up from CHF 1,066.1 bn in 2022). This represents **89.1% of total Swiss social insurance capital** (CHF 1,265 bn). Approximately **4.6 million employees** enrolled.

**The conversion rate crisis:** The statutory minimum conversion rate is **6.8%** (unchanged since 2014) — CHF 100,000 in retirement assets generates CHF 6,800 annual pension. Average rate across all funds (including supra-mandatory): **5.31%**.

- Over **CHF 90 billion redistributed** from working-age contributors to pensioners (2009–2018, PPCmetrics) — the system is already under stress.
- **BVG 21 reform** (lower conversion rate to 6.0%, lower entry threshold): **Rejected by Swiss voters on September 22, 2024.**
- Contributions by age: 7% (25–34), 10% (35–44), 15% (45–54), 18% (55–64/65). Average total: 18.4% of insured salary.

**If mass AI displacement occurs:** A 30% reduction in the employed workforce would mean ~1.4 million fewer contributors, dramatically accelerating the redistribution problem. The fully-funded model depends on both ongoing contributions and investment returns; without the former, CHF 1.1 trillion must sustain a growing pensioner population from returns alone.

### The AHV (First Pillar) Problem

Pay-as-you-go: current workers' contributions (10.6% of salary, split equally) directly fund current pensions.

**Recent reforms:**
- **AHV 21** (in force January 2024): Standardized reference age at 65 for both genders.
- **13th AHV pension:** Approved by voters March 2024; additional monthly pension in December starting 2026.
- Pensions in 2025: CHF 1,260/month (minimum) to CHF 2,520/month (maximum). Married couples capped at 150%.

**AI displacement risk:** Unlike the BVG, there is no asset pool to draw down — current contributions fund current benefits immediately. If the employed population shrinks significantly, the pay-as-you-go model faces a direct revenue crisis.

### Healthcare: Swiss Structural Advantage

Switzerland's KVG is **individually mandated, not employer-linked** — a significant structural advantage:
- Every resident must hold health insurance regardless of employment status.
- **Premium subsidies (Prämienverbilligung):** Over **2 million people** (~30% of residents) receive subsidies, income-tested by canton.
- Premium levels (2025): average CHF 378.70/month; rising 4.4% in 2026.
- Healthcare financing: 42% mandatory premiums, 26% government tax funding, 7% voluntary insurance.
- June 2024 Premium Relief Initiative (cap at 10% of income): **rejected by 56%**.
- November 2024 EFAS reform (Uniform Financing): **approved** — expected to save ~CHF 2 bn/year. Implementation begins 2028.
- **Post-work challenge:** If incomes fall due to AI displacement, the subsidy system faces dramatically higher demand. Cantonal administration creates significant variation in eligibility and generosity.

### UVG (Accident Insurance): Fully Employer-Linked

- Mandatory for all employees; employer-arranged.
- Occupational premiums: paid entirely by employer. Non-occupational: deducted from wages.
- Benefits: 80% of salary from day 3, up to CHF 148,200 maximum insured earnings.
- **When employment ends:** Coverage ceases on last day; non-occupational extends only 31 days.
- This is a pure employment-linked benefit with no individual fallback. Post-work policy needs to fold accident coverage into KVG or create a universal mechanism.

### Housing: Cooperative Expansion Potential

**Switzerland's rental market (2025):**
- **58% of dwellings** occupied by tenants; **61% of households** (2.4 million) rented.
- Vacancy rate: **1.00%** nationally (June 2025) — lowest since 2013. Geneva: 0.34%, Zurich: 0.48%.
- Average rent: ~**CHF 1,650/month** for 1-bedroom. Zurich/Geneva central: CHF 2,400–2,600/month.
- New construction: only **40,750 units** in 2024 (down 12.8% YoY).

**Housing cooperatives (Wohnbaugenossenschaften):**
- National market share: **~5% of housing stock** (~8% of rental sector).
- Rents **~20% below market average**.
- **14.3% of rental apartments in Zurich**, 12.9% in Basel.
- Over **1,200 cooperative housing providers** (WBG Schweiz). More than half Minergie-certified.
- **Combined cooperative membership:** Migros (2M) + Coop (2.67M) + Raiffeisen (2M) + housing cooperatives = ~6.67 million cooperative memberships in a country of ~9 million. One of the highest cooperative membership rates globally.

**Expansion potential:** Extremely tight rental market (1% vacancy) makes cooperative expansion both urgent and challenging. Barriers include land availability and long planning cycles. Cooperatives serve dual goals: housing affordability and community-building (satisfying SDT's relatedness need).

### Digital Infrastructure

**Swisscom Universal Service Obligation (2024–2031):**
- Mandated minimum: 10/1 Mbit/s (or 80/8 on request) for all residents.
- Broadband penetration: **46.75%** of population — top of OECD.
- FTTH coverage: ~50% of households (2024), targeting 75–80% by 2030.
- Swisscom investing ~CHF 1.7 bn/year, aiming for near-universal fibre by 2035.
- Major cities already offer multi-gigabit (up to 10 Gbps).

**Policy gap:** The universal service guarantees *access* but does not frame internet as a *right*. In a post-work society where civic participation, education, and community move online, the 10 Mbit/s floor may be inadequate.

### Sozialhilfe Capacity

Currently costs CHF 2.5 bn/year for ~280,000 recipients. A doubling or tripling of caseload would strain cantonal budgets. Designed for temporary need, not structural mass unemployment.

### Global direction:
- World Bank 2025: 2 billion people globally lack adequate social protection. Every $1 transferred to poor families generates $2.50 in local economic activity.
- The trend in progressive policy is toward universal basic services (housing, healthcare, education, transport) provided regardless of employment status.

## 6. Work Sharing & Reduced Working Hours

### Historical Precedent: Kellogg's 6-Hour Day (1930–1985)

On December 1, 1930, W.K. Kellogg replaced three 8-hour shifts with four 6-hour shifts at his Battle Creek plant. Workers received a 12.5% hourly raise (daily pay fell ~15%).

- **Productivity:** 15% more shredded wheat cases packed per hour
- **After 5 years:** Overhead costs down 25%, labor costs down 10%, accidents down 41%
- **Forbes** called it "the topic of discussion in the business world"; it was "a complete success" (*NYT*)
- Kellogg envisioned "Liberation Capitalism" — six-hour shifts would shift the balance from work to leisure

**The decline:** After WWII, cultural shifts eroded the model. In 1946, 87% of women and 71% of men voted to restore 6 hours. But by 1957 most departments returned to 8 hours. Management "denigrated and feminized" shorter hours. The last department switched in 1985. The lesson: **work-time reduction succeeds economically but requires cultural reinforcement to endure.** (Hunnicutt, *Kellogg's Six-Hour Day*, Temple University Press, 1996)

### Modern Evidence: Four-Day Week Trials

**Iceland (2015–2019):** ~2,500 workers (>1% of workforce), 35–36 hour weeks, no pay cut.
- Productivity maintained or improved. Wellbeing "dramatically increased" across stress, burnout, health, work-life balance.
- Post-trial: 86% of Iceland's workforce now working shorter hours or have the right to negotiate them.
- Iceland's labor productivity grew 1.5%/year (highest among Nordic countries).

**UK 4 Day Week Global Pilot (2022):** 61 companies, ~2,900 workers. **100-80-100 model**: 100% pay, 80% hours, 100% productivity commitment.
- 92% of companies continued; 18 made it permanent. Revenue rose 1.4% on average.
- Staff departures dropped 57%. 39% less stress; 71% reduced burnout.
- **One-year follow-up:** 89% still operating 4-day week; 51% permanently switched.

**Germany (2024):** 45 organizations. 73% planned to continue. **February 2026 follow-up:** 70% still operating reduced hours. 81% of Germans support the shift (Hans-Böckler Foundation).

**IG Metall campaigns:** In 2018, secured the right for 2.3 million members to work 28 hours/week (down from 35) for up to 2 years with guaranteed return to full-time. In 2023–24, pushed for 32-hour/4-day week in steel.

**France 35-Hour Week (Aubry Laws 1998/2000):**
- Employment rose 9.9% in firms that adopted. But IMF concluded "mainly negative impact." Total factor productivity decreased 3.7%. ~110 bn francs/year in subsidies.
- **Key lesson:** The laws have been "deconstructed stone by stone since 2005" through five successive laws allowing up to 220 hours overtime — a de facto return to 39 hours. Political sustainability requires deep cultural buy-in.

**Spain (2023–2027):** EUR 50 million EU-funded pilot targeting ~200 firms for 32-hour weeks. Government covers 100%→50%→33% of costs over 3 years.

### Swiss Context

**Zurich 35-hour trial (2023+):** City parliament approved (60–57 vote) for shift workers — sanitation, police, transport. Estimated cost if extended to all departments: **CHF 110 million** and ~1,500 additional staff. "Who, if not the rich city of Zurich, can afford to try the 35-hour week?" — Councilor David Garcia Nunez.

**Swiss part-time culture:**
- **38.7% of employed persons** work part-time (Q2 2024), up 13.3pp since 1991.
- Part-time almost **3x more common among women** (59%) than men (28%).
- Full-time average: **40 hours 4 minutes/week** (2024), down 50 minutes from 2019.
- All employed (including part-time): ~1,557 hours/year (OECD), equivalent to ~30–35h/week.
- **Kurzarbeit:** Proven at scale (COVID: 1.3M workers, CHF 11 bn/year). But designed for temporary shocks, not permanent displacement.

### Economics of Hours Reduction

- **Portugal (44→40 hours):** 6% increase in hourly wages, ~2% employment decline, 9% total hours decline. Firm-level labor demand elasticity: -1.48 for hours, -0.33 for employment.
- **Hours-productivity relationship:** Reducing hours raises hourly productivity (fatigue channel), with elasticity estimates of **-0.4 to -0.6**.
- **Systematic review (2023):** Work-time reductions show small positive employment effects and unclear productivity outcomes; generally positive effects on wellbeing, health, and work-family conflict.
- **The wage compensation question:** Reductions with full wage compensation need subsidies to prevent employment drops. The 100-80-100 model works for knowledge workers but may not transfer to all sectors.

### Degrowth Connection

**Tim Jackson** (*Prosperity Without Growth*, 2009/2017): Beyond a certain point, growth does not increase wellbeing. The "post-growth" economy requires transforming working lives and investment. Endorsed by Chomsky and Varoufakis.

**Jason Hickel** (*Less is More*, 2020): Degrowth is about reducing material throughput, not GDP per se. Key policy: shorten the working week to maintain full employment while reducing throughput. People with more free time use it for family, volunteering, health — not more consumption — and emit less GHG.

### Job Guarantee (Tcherneva, Wray — MMT School)

**Core proposal:** Government as employer of last resort, offering a voluntary public service job at a living wage (~$15/hour + benefits). Functions as a "buffer stock" — expands in recessions, shrinks as private demand recovers.

- **Eliminates the NAIRU trade-off** between unemployment and inflation.
- Whatever wage the JG provides becomes the effective economy-wide minimum.
- **Precedent:** Argentina's *Jefes y Jefas* employed 13% of the labor force within months.
- **Macroeconomic estimates** (Fullwiler, Fair model): Employing 11–16 million → permanently increase private employment by 4 million and real GDP by $313–560 bn/year.

**Swiss adaptation:** The existing Zivildienst framework could serve as a nucleus. However, the government is actively *restricting* access (at SVP's urging) rather than expanding it. The debt brake constrains fiscal space for large-scale programs.

## 7. Swiss-Specific Policy Landscape

### What Switzerland already has
| Tool | Status | Readiness for mass unemployment |
|------|--------|-------------------------------|
| ALV (unemployment insurance) | 70–80% replacement, 18 months | ❌ Designed for temporary unemployment |
| Kurzarbeit (short-time work) | Proven at scale (COVID) | ⚠️ Temporary shock only |
| Sozialhilfe (social assistance) | Cantonal, needs-based | ❌ Not scaled for mass caseload |
| AHV (state pension) | Universal, 13th pension from 2026 | ✅ Not employment-dependent |
| KVG (health insurance) | Mandatory individual, with subsidies | ✅ Not employer-linked |
| BVG (occupational pension) | Employer-linked | ❌ Vulnerable to mass unemployment |
| VET (vocational training) | World-class dual-track system | ⚠️ Needs mid/late-career extension |
| Cantonal minimum wages | 5 cantons, CHF 19–24.50/hour | ⚠️ Irrelevant if no jobs exist |

### Structural Constraints on Swiss AI Policy

**The Debt Brake (Schuldenbremse) — Article 126 BV:**
- Enshrined in the Constitution since 2001, approved by **85% of voters**.
- The Confederation may only spend as much as it collects, adjusted for the economic cycle.
- Results: Debt ratio fell from **25.3% (2003) to 13.5% (2019)**.
- **COVID test:** CHF 30 billion mobilized within weeks — but must be repaid, constraining future fiscal space.
- **Constraint on AI policy:** Severely limits large-scale counter-cyclical spending (job guarantees, expanded safety nets, retraining). Any sustained program needs either extraordinary budget approval (absolute majority + 6-year payback) or a constitutional amendment (double majority referendum).

**SNB at the Zero Bound:**
- Policy rate **0.00% since June 2025** after six consecutive cuts from 1.75%.
- Negative rate experience (2015–2022): -0.75% for seven years, effective rate only ~-0.3% due to exemptions. Limited macro stimulus; paradoxically, mortgage rates *increased*. Financial services lost 16% of jobs (20,500 FTE).
- SNB balance sheet peaked at **~120% of GDP** (~CHF 1 trillion). 2022 loss: CHF 132 billion (16.3% of GDP).
- **Essentially no conventional monetary stimulus remaining** if AI displacement triggers a deflationary demand shock.

**Cantonal Fiscal Autonomy:**
- 26 cantons manage nearly **25% of total fiscal income** — among the greatest subnational fiscal autonomy globally.
- Tax competition: Zug ~11.8% corporate rate vs. Geneva >40% effective income tax for high earners.
- **Any national AI displacement policy faces cantonal resistance** if it requires harmonized fiscal measures.
- **Opportunity:** Cantons function as "laboratories of democracy" — 26 parallel policy experiments. Successful experiments scale nationally; failed ones are geographically contained.

**Cross-Border Workers (Grenzgänger):**
- Record **411,000 G-permit holders** by end of 2025 (7.6% of workforce). 58% from France, 22% Italy, 16% Germany.
- **Geneva:** 114,700 cross-border workers. **Ticino:** ~79,000. **Basel:** ~74,000.
- Border regions are highly vulnerable to AI displacement — their economies are deeply intertwined with cross-border labor supply.
- **Bilaterals III safeguard clause** (signed March 2026): allows Switzerland to temporarily restrict free movement if predefined thresholds exceeded — potentially relevant to AI scenarios. Still faces referendum.

**Direct Democracy Timeline:**
- Popular initiative: 100,000 signatures, 18 months collection, double majority required. Only **26 of ~200+ accepted** since 1891.
- Optional referendum: 50,000 signatures, 100 days, simple majority. ~40% of challenged laws rejected.
- **5–10 years from idea to implementation** — may be too slow for rapid AI displacement.

**Neutrality and Multilateral Constraints:**
- OECD membership constrains capital movement rules. WTO/GATS binds Swiss financial services commitments.
- Bilaterals III requires dynamic alignment with EU rules through Joint Committee decisions, annual payments of CHF 350 million (2030–2036).
- **Any AI policy involving import restrictions, local-content requirements, or discriminatory subsidies faces WTO constraints.**

### What Switzerland doesn't have (and may need)
1. **Structural unemployment insurance** — ALV extension or new scheme for permanent displacement beyond 18 months
2. **Universal basic services** — Free/subsidized housing, transport, digital access as rights
3. **Funded civic participation infrastructure** — Paid community roles, structured volunteering with social recognition
4. **BVG reform** — Pension continuity mechanism for permanently displaced workers
5. **Scaled mental health infrastructure** — Community-based, preventive, accessible without employment
6. **Purpose institutions** — Lifelong learning centers, maker spaces, community hubs that provide structure/identity
7. **Social Purpose Market mechanisms** — Incentivizing care, learning, civic participation as valued economic activity
8. **AI-specific revenue mechanisms** — SWF, data royalties, or automation levies to fund the transition

### AI regulation approach
Switzerland has opted for a *lean regulatory approach* — no general AI law like the EU AI Act. Instead: ratification of Council of Europe AI Convention + sector-specific measures. No specific policy linking AI displacement to social safety net reform as of March 2026.

## 8. Democratic Legitimacy Under Mass Unemployment

### Work as the Primary Locus of Democratic Participation

**Carole Pateman's Spillover Hypothesis** (*Participation and Democratic Theory*, 1970): Workplace participation educates citizens in democratic practices. Weber et al. (2020) meta-analysis confirmed: employees in more democratic workplaces show increased moral/community orientation and higher political participation.

**The OECD 2024 Trust Survey:** Only **30% of OECD respondents** feel the political system lets them have a say. 44% have low or no trust in national government. Trust deficit strongly correlated with economic insecurity.

### Arendt's Warning: The Rise of the Social

In *The Human Condition*, Arendt warns that modernity allowed economic concerns to colonize the public realm, transforming citizens into "laborers and jobholders." The "victory of *animal laborans*" means all values — permanence, stability, freedom — are sacrificed for productivity. When the economic sphere shrinks through AI displacement, **two contradictory outcomes are possible:**
1. Citizens are freed from labor to engage in genuine political *action* (Arendt's ideal)
2. Citizens lose even the degraded form of public engagement that economic life provided, falling into isolation and powerlessness

Arendt identified isolation and loneliness as the preconditions for authoritarianism (*The Origins of Totalitarianism*).

### Historical Evidence: Unemployment and Democratic Breakdown

**Weimar Republic (1929–1933):**
- Unemployment: ~2M (1926) → **6M (1932)** — nearly 30% of workforce. Wages fell 39%.
- Nazi vote: 2.6% (1928) → **37.3% (July 1932)**.
- Emergency rule via Article 48 replaced parliament: 5 presidential decrees (1930), 44 (1931), 60 (1932).
- **Nuance:** CEPR (2023) found areas with direct export shocks saw *declines* in Nazi support — the link operated through indirect, complex mechanisms, not simple joblessness.

**Contemporary: The "China Shock" → Political Polarization (Autor, Dorn, Hanson & Majlesi, AER 2020):**
- Trade-exposed communities showed strong correlation with Trump voting in 2016.
- Manufacturing job losses produced Fox News market share increases, ideological polarization, and election of more extreme politicians.
- **Racial polarization:** Majority-White trade-exposed counties elected more conservative GOP; majority-minority counties elected more liberal Democrats — both at the expense of moderates.
- "Adverse economic shocks appear to abet the election of more extreme politicians by encouraging voters to separate according to group identity."

**Brexit and deindustrialisation:** 64% of C2/DE (manual) voters voted Leave vs. 43% of AB voters. Old industrial areas voted Leave at rates above the 53% England/Wales average. Education, not deprivation alone, was the strongest predictor — but the "left behind" narrative captures a real phenomenon.

- **Greece 2010s:** 58% youth unemployment → collapse of establishment, rise of Syriza and Golden Dawn.
- **Spain 2010s:** 55% youth unemployment → 18% widening of income distribution.
- **Global 2025:** International IDEA issued 20 democratic alerts in 4 months — 2x the rate of any previous full year.

### Swiss-Specific Resilience — and Risks

**Direct democracy as pressure valve:** Switzerland's system allows citizens to channel discontent through initiatives and referendums rather than extreme party formation. ~700 federal popular votes since 1848. V-Dem rated Switzerland the most participatory democratic country in 2024. ~90% of citizens participated in at least one vote within a four-year period.

**But: participation challenges:**
- Average turnout ~49.7% (5-year average); controversial issues reach 60%+.
- **Gemeindeversammlungen** under-represent 20–40 year-olds and women. Research shows communes that switched from assembly to parliament saw 6% spending increases — assemblies failed to represent the full population.
- **Participation skews toward older, wealthier, more educated citizens** — precisely those *least likely* to face AI displacement.
- **Key risk:** If AI displacement primarily affects younger, less wealthy, less educated workers, the populations most impacted would be least represented in the democratic process determining the policy response.

### Deliberative Democracy Innovations

**Ireland's Citizens' Assemblies:** Two constitutional amendments (marriage equality, Eighth Amendment repeal) originated from deliberative exercises — 99 randomly selected citizens deliberating over 19 months. World leader in this form.

**Swiss Citizens' Assembly (2025):** Run by Universities of Zurich and Geneva. 22,000 people invited from BFS census sample; 2,004 registered. Met over three weekends plus four online sessions. Recommended health-promoting reforms.

**Belgium's permanent Citizens' Council:** The German-speaking community (80,000 inhabitants) installed a permanent Citizens' Council and Assembly in 2019 as a *third democratic institution* alongside Parliament and Executive.

**AI-assisted democracy (Nature Human Behaviour 2025; Carnegie Endowment 2026):**
- AI enables "automated hyper-personalization" creating quasi-governing corporate power without democratic legitimacy.
- But AI also offers tools to "educate and learn from citizens, strengthen public discourse, help people find common ground."
- Generative AI could make citizen-initiated mechanisms cheaper and more frequent — but risks "converting these mechanisms into engines of plebiscitarian instability."

### Policy implications for high-unemployment society:
- **Direct democracy as buffer:** Switzerland's direct democracy may be uniquely resilient — citizens *are* the government. But the system must ensure displaced workers are *actively* included.
- **Expand deliberative models:** Swiss Citizens' Assembly pilot could become a permanent institution for AI governance deliberation.
- **Link reduced hours to civic participation:** A reformed, expanded Zivildienst model — though the November 2025 vote (84% against compulsory civic duty) shows headwinds.
- **Build on militia tradition:** If regular employment shrinks, the militia *Milizsystem* could be extended into a broader "civic participation" framework.

## 9. Physical Infrastructure for Abundant Free Time

### The built environment for a post-work society

If millions of people have vastly more free time, what physical spaces do they need? Current urban design assumes people spend 8+ hours at workplaces.

### Community Centres and Cultural Spaces

**Swiss Gemeinschaftszentren:** Zurich operates **17 city-funded community centres**, open to all for free — playgrounds, cafes, workshops, studios, resident-initiated programming. Switzerland's tradition dates to the Tscharnergut centre, inspired by Dutch, Swedish, and Finnish models.

**Exemplary cultural spaces:**
- **Rote Fabrik (Zurich):** Former silk mill, collectively run since 1987. 100+ concerts/year, 60 art studios, international artist-in-residence programme.
- **Bains des Pâquis (Geneva):** Association-run, CHF 2 entry — swimming, spa, restaurant with Mont Blanc views. Low-barrier social gathering.
- **Kornhausforum (Bern):** Former granary — exhibitions and public discussions on design, photography, urban culture.

**Makerspaces and Fab Labs:** Swiss FabLabs network (fablab.ch) promotes MIT's Fab Lab concept. FabLab Zurich (laser cutters, CNC, 3D printers, electronics, woodworking). Les Ateliers de Renens (old printing factory → creative cluster with makerspace, fablab, bio-hacklab, coworking). 2,300+ Fab Labs globally.

**Co-working:** 72+ listed spaces in Switzerland. Second-highest average hot desk price globally ($395/month, after Monaco).

### Sports and Recreation

**Swiss sports infrastructure:**
- **~19,000 sports clubs** with 2 million licensed members. Swiss Olympic umbrella: 116 federations, ~2.5 million active members, 18,440 clubs.
- **71% of the population** regularly engages in physical activity. A quarter of residents aged 5–74 belong to a club.
- **32,000 sports facilities** nationally. Sport accounts for **1.7% of GDP** — EUR 22.7 bn annual turnover.
- **40+ international sports federations** headquartered in Switzerland, adding CHF 1 bn+ annually.

**Hiking:** 65,000+ km of marked trails, protected by **Article 88 of the Federal Constitution**. Maintenance: ~CHF 800/km/year (~CHF 50M total), largely municipal with ~2,000 volunteers. **2.7 million regular hikers** (44% of population), averaging 20 excursions/year. 80% use the network at least occasionally.

**Skiing:** 2,400 lifts. 22.3M skier days (2022/23). Revenues: CHF 837M. One-third of residents ski regularly — second-highest proportion in Europe.

### Cultural Infrastructure

**Federal cultural spending:** CHF 988 million for 2025–2028 (~CHF 247M/year). Total public cultural expenditure: **CHF 352 per capita** (0.42% of GDP). Culture accounts for ~10.4% of all Swiss companies, representing 268,000 jobs.

### Transport: Repurposing for Reduced Commuting

**SBB ridership (2025):** Record **1.43 million passengers daily** (up from 1.39M in 2024). Punctuality: 94.1%. Profit: CHF 496M.

**If AI reduces commuting:** The dense rail network could be repurposed for expanded leisure/tourism travel (already growing on weekends), more evenly distributed ridership, and more frequent off-peak services. CHF 500M annual profit suggests financial capacity for adaptation.

### Urban Planning: Verdichtung and the 15-Minute City

**Swiss Verdichtung (Densification):** Since the 2014 Federal Spatial Planning Act revision (accepted 62.9% of voters, March 2013): "inward development before outward development." All cantons/municipalities legally obligated to direct settlement inward. ETH Zurich's SPUR group evaluating every spatial planning referendum from 2002–2020 across 162 cities (SNSF "Densifying Switzerland" project).

**15-Minute City (Carlos Moreno, 2016):** Density, proximity, diversity, digitalization — ensuring residents access living, work, commerce, healthcare, education, entertainment within 15-minute walk/bike. Swiss Verdichtung closely mirrors these principles.

### Libraries as the Model

- Libraries are already evolving into the community hubs a post-work society needs: free, open to all, offering maker spaces, media production, WiFi, social connection, and creative resources.
- Eric Klinenberg (*Palaces for the People*): libraries have the greatest potential for connecting people and fighting inequality, polarization, and civic decline.

### The "atrophied muscle" problem

Research shows that currently, away from work, people mainly "watch TV, try to sleep, and in general vegetate." The *Marienthal* study (Section 2) found library usage *fell* despite unlimited free time. Investment in structured but voluntary activity spaces helps rebuild the capacity for self-directed activity.

### Estimated Investment Needs

No single estimate exists for a comprehensive "leisure infrastructure buildout." Data points for extrapolation:
- **Trail maintenance:** CHF 50M/year currently (65,000 km).
- **Swisscom fibre:** CHF 1.7 bn/year until 2035.
- **Federal culture:** ~CHF 247M/year (only ~32% of total public cultural spend).
- **Sport infrastructure:** 32,000 facilities generating EUR 22.7 bn turnover — maintenance/expansion requires multi-billion annual investment.
- **Community centres:** Zurich's 17 centres serve ~400,000 residents. Scaling nationally for 3.5M+ with significantly more free time: potentially CHF 1–3 bn in capital expenditure.

**Rough aggregate:** A serious "leisure infrastructure" buildout accommodating 20–40% workforce displacement would likely require **CHF 3–7 billion in additional annual investment** across digital, cultural, recreational, and community infrastructure, on top of existing spending.

## 10. Historical Lessons: Societies That Lived With High Unemployment

### Industrial Revolution (1780–1850): The First Great Transition

The first Industrial Revolution spanned ~1760–1840, with acute social disruption concentrated 1800–1850. **The full transition took 60–80 years.**

**The Luddites (1811–1816):** Skilled textile workers who destroyed machinery threatening livelihoods. Not anti-technology per se — advocating for workers' rights and job security. Government deployed **12,000 troops** to protect factories — more than were fighting Napoleon in Spain.

**Chartism (1838–1858):** First mass working-class political movement — petitions signed by millions. Though immediately unsuccessful, five of six Charter demands were eventually enacted. Demonstrated that channelling grievances into political demands serves as a pressure-release valve.

**What prevented social collapse:** Gradual franchise expansion (Reform Acts 1832, 1867, 1884); Factory Acts; trade union legalization; channelling revolutionary energy into reformist movements; and a minimal welfare safety net (Poor Laws).

**Key lesson: The transition took 60–80 years with acute disruption lasting 2–3 decades. Collapse was avoided through gradual political inclusion and incremental welfare provision.**

### The Great Depression (1929–1939)

**U.S.:** Peak 24.9% unemployment (1933), 12.8M people. Wage income fell 42.5%. Social devastation: bank failures, "Hoovervilles," family breakdown.

**The New Deal:** Created a fundamentally new citizen-government relationship. Financial regulation (1933–34) heralded decades of stability. Jobs programmes and welfare provision generated "faith in the federal government" lasting until the 1960s.

**Switzerland's 1930s:**
- National income fell nearly 20%; recovery not until 1937.
- Banking crisis: Schweizerische Volksbank rescued with CHF 100M rights issue (~¼ of national budget).
- Deflationary orthodoxy and gold standard (until 1936) deepened the recession.
- Less than a third of working men and a fifth of women insured against unemployment.
- **The Friedensabkommen (Peace Agreement) of 19 July 1937:** Between Konrad Ilg (metalworkers' union) and Ernst Dübi (engineering employers). Five pages stipulating absolute industrial peace and multi-stage arbitration. Each party deposited CHF 250,000 with the SNB as guarantee.
- **Context:** Ilg feared prolonged strikes would boost right-wing extremists given the European authoritarian zeitgeist.
- **Legacy:** Transformed Swiss labour relations from "class war" into cooperative *Sozialpartnerschaft*. Strikes became extremely rare. Renewed repeatedly; basis for the full GAV from 1974. Original document displayed at the Swiss National Museum.

**Political divergence:** Same crisis → New Deal (US), centrist coalition (UK), fascist tyranny (Germany), pragmatic social partnership (Switzerland). **Institutional resilience and leadership determined outcomes.**

### Post-WWII: The Beveridge Settlement

**Beveridge Report (1942):** Five giants — Want, Disease, Ignorance, Squalor, Idleness. Universal benefits "from cradle to grave." Best-selling British government document of the 20th century.

**Post-War Consensus (1945–1979):** Multi-partisan agreement on welfare state, NHS, mixed economy, Keynesian macroeconomics, full employment. T.H. Marshall argued these measures represented the "culmination of social citizenship rights" and a viable democratic alternative to Soviet communism.

**Key lesson: Success because it was comprehensive (addressing multiple "giants" simultaneously), enjoyed cross-party consensus, and was embedded in broader international economic cooperation (Marshall Plan).**

### 1970s–80s Deindustrialisation

**UK Miners' Strike (1984–85):** 200,000+ miners mobilised for a year. After defeat: most mines closed, 50% unemployment in mining communities, lasting social damage. Scotland's Just Transition Commission (2021): "an example of how not to manage structural change." In contrast, the careful 1960s–70s coal transition managed losses through alternative employment — "the moral economy in operation."

**US Rust Belt:** Decline from 1950s. NBER study of 1,993 cities across six countries: cities with higher college-educated shares recovered more. Pittsburgh (Carnegie Mellon, U of Pittsburgh) is the success story. **49% of German hubs recovered vs. only 17% of US hubs.**

**Swiss Watch Crisis (Quarzkrise, 1970s–80s):**
- Employment: 90,000 → 28,000 (1970–1988) — two-thirds lost.
- Mechanical exports: 40M (1973) → 3M (1983). Market share: 50%+ → 24%.
- Recovery through 1983 ASUAG/SSIH merger (Swatch Group), led by Nicolas Hayek — repositioned from mass-market to luxury.
- **Switzerland's closest analogue to AI displacement.** Recovery required visionary private-sector leadership, not government intervention. But the human cost was enormous and geographically concentrated.

### German Reunification (1990s): Shock Therapy

**Treuhandanstalt:** Privatised 8,500 companies with 4+ million employees. 2.5 million of 4 million laid off. Accumulated DM 260–270 billion debt by 1994 closure.

**Persistent divergence:** Household wealth remained 2x higher in West (EUR 94,000 vs 40,000 per adult in 2014). Ownership gap — most productive firms sold to West German investors. Fuelled AfD support.

**Solidaritätszuschlag:** 5.5% surcharge, introduced 1991, still in force 2025 (declared constitutional March 2025). Raises EUR 13 bn/year. **Total cost of reunification: EUR 1.5–2 trillion.**

**Key lesson: Even massive transfers (€1.5–2T) cannot overcome shock-therapy damage if the social contract is violated.**

### China Shock (2000s)

**Autor, Dorn & Hanson:** Chinese import competition eliminated **2.0–2.4 million** US manufacturing jobs (1999–2011) — 59.3% of all US manufacturing job losses (2001–2019). Displaced workers did NOT move to new regions or industries. Effects persisted two decades. "Alongside the heralded consumer benefits of expanded trade are substantial adjustment costs." **The scholars explicitly warn that AI, energy transition, and automation will produce repeated similar shocks.**

### Greece (2010–2018) and Spain (2008–2018)

**Greece:** Youth unemployment 58.1% (2012). Austerity *worsened* the crisis. Collapse of establishment parties, rise of Syriza/Golden Dawn, mass emigration.

**Spain:** Youth unemployment 55% (2012). 25.7% purchasing power loss for under-35s. 18% widening of income inequality (largest in Europe). Youth Guarantee poorly implemented.

### Cross-Cutting Lessons

**What made transitions more cohesive:**
1. **Gradual over shock** — careful 1960s–70s UK coal transition vs. Thatcher's abrupt 1980s closures
2. **Strong institutions and social dialogue** — Swiss Friedensabkommen; German codetermination; Beveridge consensus
3. **Human capital investment** — educated cities recovered from deindustrialisation (Pittsburgh); Germany's 49% vs US 17%
4. **Workers' voice in transition** — Scotland's "moral economy" framework
5. **Comprehensive welfare provision** — addressing multiple needs simultaneously
6. **Fiscal commitment** — even massive spending can't overcome shock therapy (German reunification)

**What made transitions worse:**
1. Shock therapy without consultation (Treuhandanstalt, Thatcher)
2. Assumption that markets self-correct quickly (China shock disproves this)
3. Geographic concentration of impact without targeted regional policy
4. Deflationary orthodoxy that prolongs pain (Switzerland 1930s)
5. Austerity during mass unemployment (Greece, 1937 US)

## 11. Swiss-Specific Assets: Direct Democracy, Vereine, Cooperatives & Militia System

### Why Switzerland may be uniquely resilient

Switzerland has five cultural-institutional assets that could serve as foundations for a high-unemployment society — if deliberately strengthened.

### 1. Direct Democracy

**Scale:** ~700 federal popular votes since 1848. Voting ~4 times/year on multiple issues. V-Dem rated Switzerland the **most participatory democratic country in the world** (2024). ~90% of citizens participated in at least one vote within a four-year period.

**Acceptance rates:** 72% of mandatory referendums adopted (174 of 240, 1848–2021). Only 26 popular initiatives accepted since 1891.

**Post-work relevance:** Direct democracy provides a civic framework for meaningful engagement entirely outside the labour market. The skills involved — deliberation, information processing, collective decision-making — are transferable to a post-work civic identity. 700+ votes have created deep "civic muscle memory."

### 2. Vereine (Voluntary Associations)

**Scale:** Estimated **100,000+** associations nationwide. ~55% of residents aged 16+ participate in at least one Verein (BFS).

**Sports clubs:** 18,440 under Swiss Olympic with ~2.5 million active members. Active membership has remained stable but clubs have not benefited from population growth. Voluntary work remains the foundation and the #1 challenge.

**"Vereinsmeier" culture:** The term captures Switzerland's distinctive culture of organised collective life outside work and state. The Verein is used for everything from professional governance to neighbourhood clubs to international sports federations.

**Volunteering rates:** 41% formal, 51% informal. Volunteers have higher institutional trust and are more likely to participate in direct democracy. 55% reject "I have no say in government" vs. 36% of non-volunteers.

**Post-work relevance:** The Verein infrastructure provides ready-made structures for purposeful collective activity outside employment — satisfying SDT's autonomy, competence, and relatedness needs. If work diminishes, Switzerland already has extensive "non-work" institutional infrastructure for social belonging.

### 3. Cooperative Tradition

**Scale:** ~8,100 cooperatives in the trade register. Top ten cooperatives generated **10.6% of Switzerland's economic output** (2022).

**Major cooperatives:**
- **Migros:** ~2 million cooperative members (founded 1925, Gottlieb Duttweiler). 90,000+ employees, CHF 32+ bn sales. 10 regional cooperatives. Free membership for every adult.
- **Coop:** ~2.67 million members. 97,275 employees, CHF 35.5 bn sales. Membership CHF 15/year.
- **Raiffeisen:** ~2 million members (co-owners of 212 independent banks). 3.75 million customers, 896 branches — densest network in Switzerland.
- **Housing cooperatives:** ~170,000 units (~5% of stock). Zurich city: 18–25% cooperative; rents 20–40% below market. City goal: one-third non-profit housing by 2050.

**Combined membership:** Migros + Coop + Raiffeisen = **~6.67 million** cooperative memberships in a country of ~9 million. One of the highest cooperative membership rates globally.

**Post-work relevance:** Cooperatives offer a governance model for economic activity that doesn't depend on employer-employee relationships. Could be expanded to manage community resources, energy, care, food systems, and digital infrastructure through democratic, member-owned organisations.

### 4. The Militia System (Milizsystem)

**Constitutional basis:** Article 58 BV (military militia); broader civic tradition from 1798 Helvetic Constitution ("Every citizen is a born soldier of the Fatherland").

**Scope:** Citizen soldiers, citizen politicians, citizen firefighters. Federal Assembly is nominally a militia parliament. At communal level, militia members form legislatures and sometimes executives.

**Crisis:** Willingness to serve has declined throughout the 21st century. Greater individualism, mobility, demanding work environments. Only small-to-medium municipalities still use militia at executive level. Gender gap: 1.7% men vs. 0.5% women in political roles (2020). Civic Duty Initiative (November 2025) — extend service to all citizens — rejected 84%.

**Post-work paradox:** The current crisis stems partly from overworked professionals lacking time. If 20–40% of the workforce has substantially more free time, this bottleneck eases. The militia model could expand into a broader "civic service" framework — community governance, environmental stewardship, social care, cultural production.

### 5. Concordance (Konkordanz) and Federalism

**Concordance:** All four major parties share the seven Federal Council seats ("magic formula"). "Decisions are lasting only if they are supported by the minority as well as the majority." No permanent opposition. Consensus reduces winner-take-all dynamics.

**Post-work relevance:** AI displacement creates intense distributional conflicts (who benefits from AI? who receives transfers?). Winner-take-all politics risks capture by either beneficiaries or displaced. Concordance forces all sides to the table, making negotiated compromise the default.

**Federalism as resilience:** 26 cantons with own constitutions, legislatures, governments. 2,100+ municipalities. Cantons/municipalities manage ~68% of public expenditure. **Different cantons could experiment with different AI displacement responses** — varying UBI designs, retraining approaches, civic service models, tax structures. This "option value" of 26 parallel experiments is a structural advantage over centralized states.

**Eigenverantwortung (Personal Responsibility — Article 6 BV):** "Every person is responsible for themselves and contributes according to their abilities." Deeply ingrained in Swiss-German mentality. **The concept faces a fundamental challenge when the labour market cannot absorb willing workers.** It would need to evolve from "personal responsibility = employment" toward "personal responsibility = contribution through diverse channels" (care, civic service, cultural production, cooperative governance).

### The challenge:
- The militia system is under pressure: work-life demands make part-time civic office burdensome.
- If volunteering doesn't increase, social cohesion "will crumble in the long term."
- Cooperative membership is high but active governance participation is lower — most are passive members.
- Paradoxically, mass unemployment could *help* — more free time means more capacity for civic engagement, *if* the infrastructure and incentives exist.

## Summary: A Toolkit for Living With High Unemployment

The paradigm shift is: **stop trying only to keep people employed — start building the infrastructure for people to live well without traditional employment.**

| Domain | Tool | Function |
|--------|------|----------|
| **Income** | UBI / Guaranteed Income | Material floor regardless of employment |
| **Income** | Sovereign Wealth Fund / AI dividends | Public ownership of automated economy's returns |
| **Income** | AI/automation taxation | Fund social infrastructure from productivity gains |
| **Income** | Universal Basic Services | Free healthcare, education, housing, transport |
| **Health** | Universal healthcare (CH: already exists) | Decouple health from employment |
| **Mental health** | Community mental health infrastructure | Address identity/purpose crisis at scale |
| **Purpose** | Social Purpose Markets / "Virtue Labor" | Incentivize care, learning, civic work |
| **Purpose** | Expanded Militia System / civic roles | Scale Switzerland's existing civic participation |
| **Purpose** | Lifelong learning as social institution | Education as meaning, not just vocational |
| **Purpose** | Strengthened Vereine (associations) | Scale existing social infrastructure |
| **Housing** | Universal basic housing / expanded social housing | Decouple shelter from income |
| **Pensions** | BVG reform / universal pension expansion | Close employer-linked gap |
| **Work sharing** | Reduced hours / 4-day week | Distribute remaining work broadly |
| **Work sharing** | Job Guarantee | Government as employer of last resort |
| **Physical infra** | Libraries, maker spaces, community hubs | Built environment for abundant free time |
| **Physical infra** | Parks, plazas, sports, community gardens | Public spaces as essential (not luxury) infrastructure |
| **Democracy** | Direct democracy (CH: already exists) | Maintain legitimacy when jobs can't |
| **Democracy** | Expanded civic participation rights | Give economically excluded citizens voice and agency |
| **Social cohesion** | Community hubs, local institutions | Build belonging outside workplace |
| **Identity** | Cultural shift in success metrics | Redefine value beyond professional achievement |

### The central question for the simulator

The existing labourshift tool models a consumption-collapse spiral and tests counter-measures that try to reduce unemployment. The new direction would model **societal resilience infrastructure**: what investments in housing, healthcare, community, income support, and democratic participation are needed to maintain quality of life and social stability at various unemployment levels (10%, 15%, 20%, 25%+)?

Key modelling variables:
- **Social cohesion index** as a function of: income floor, community infrastructure investment, civic participation rates, mental health service capacity
- **Democratic stability index** as a function of: unemployment rate, income inequality (Gini), institutional trust, direct democratic participation
- **Wellbeing index** as a function of: purpose activities (volunteering, learning, care), physical infrastructure, social connection, health access
- **Fiscal sustainability** as a function of: AI-derived revenue (SWF dividends, automation tax), reduced traditional tax base, social spending requirements
